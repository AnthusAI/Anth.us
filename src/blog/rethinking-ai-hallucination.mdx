## Do you use spell checking?

<BlogImage images={props.pageContext.frontmatter.images} name="autocorrect-fails.jpg" className="right" alt="Autocorrect fails." />

Let's take a step back and consider something we use every day: spell-check. We've all had that moment when spell-check suggests a wildly inappropriate word. Maybe it tried to change "meeting" to "meatball" or "presentation" to "fermentation." But do we throw up our hands and swear off spell-check forever? Of course not. We chuckle, ignore the odd suggestion, and keep on typing.

In many ways, AI hallucinations are like those quirky spell-check suggestions. They're not errors to be eliminated, but rather side effects of a powerful tool trying to make sense of ambiguous or incomplete information.

What we call "hallucinations" in AI are often the model's attempt to reconcile missing or incomplete information. This ability is crucial for the AI to provide useful responses without constantly stopping to ask for clarification.

Imagine if every time you asked an AI a question, it responded with a list of a hundred clarifying questions before it could answer. That wouldn't be very helpful, would it? Instead, the AI makes educated guesses to fill in the gaps, allowing it to provide a coherent and often useful response.

## The Power of Filling in the Gaps

One of the most valuable features of large language models (LLMs) is their ability to work with incomplete information. Far from being a flaw, this capability allows AI to handle a wide range of tasks with minimal input, making it flexible and adaptable in real-world situations where perfect information is rarely available.

Without this, LLMs would require painstakingly specific details before they could provide any useful responses, turning simple tasks into frustrating, time-consuming interactions.

To illustrate how this feature improves the user experience, let's compare two different approaches. In the first, the LLM is able to fill in missing information on its own, generating a seamless and helpful response. In the second, the LLM requires every detail to be provided before it can proceed, leading to an exasperating back-and-forth.

## Harnessing AI's Reconciliation Ability

To leverage AI's ability to reconcile incomplete information, we need to fundamentally shift our approach. This starts with understanding the nature of AI responses. When we interact with an AI, we're not simply retrieving pre-stored information; we're engaging with a system that's actively trying to make sense of our queries, often by making educated guesses to fill in missing details.

This understanding should inform how we use and interpret AI outputs. Rather than treating AI responses as definitive answers, we should view them as starting points for further investigation. This approach requires us to sharpen our critical evaluation skills. We need to become adept at identifying where an AI might be filling in gaps and verify crucial information when accuracy is paramount.

For organizations integrating AI into their workflows, this means investing in comprehensive training programs. These programs should go beyond simple tool use, focusing on developing employees' ability to work alongside AI effectively. This includes teaching staff how to recognize when an AI is making educated guesses, how to prompt the AI for more accurate responses, and how to verify critical information.

Implementing robust verification processes is crucial, especially in fields where accuracy is non-negotiable. However, these processes shouldn't be seen as a way to "catch" AI mistakes, but rather as a collaborative step in the human-AI workflow. By combining AI's ability to quickly process and connect vast amounts of information with human expertise and critical thinking, we can achieve results that neither could accomplish alone.

## Bridging Information Gaps: The True Power of AI

As we continue to integrate AI into various aspects of our work and lives, it's crucial to approach it with a balanced perspective. What we've often viewed as "hallucinations" are, in reality, an AI's attempt to provide useful output even when faced with incomplete information. This feature is what makes AI so versatile and powerful across a wide range of applications.

Recent research suggests that these so-called hallucinations can be categorized into different types, each with its own characteristics and potential benefits.
